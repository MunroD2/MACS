Mental Activities Card Sort (MACS) - Counsellor Instructions

Background

The Mental Activities Card Sort (MACS) is a reflexive decision-making tool developed from Margaret Archer's research on internal conversations and expanded through research with unpaid female carers considering higher education. This activity helps clients visualise and understand the complexity of their internal decision-making processes.

Research shows that clients often describe their inner conversations as "chaotic," "a big tangled mess," or "scrambled," leaving them feeling overwhelmed and unable to see things clearly (Munro et al., 2024). The MACS transforms these invisible mental processes into tangible, discussable elements, enabling clients to gain insights about their decision-making patterns and identify which mental activities are helping or hindering their progress.

Instructions

Step 1: Introduction and Exploration (15-20 minutes)

  • Present the 20 mental activity cards to your client
  • Invite them to browse through the cards and select any that resonate with them
  • Encourage deep discussion about each selected card:
    • "Tell me about this mental activity - when do you notice yourself doing this?"
    • "What's happening in your mind when you engage in this activity?"
    • "Why do you think you find yourself doing this?"
  • Note: Cards flip over to reveal definitions if clarification is needed
  • Allow clients to spend as much time as they wish discussing each activity

Step 2: Sorting Process (10-15 minutes)

  • Click "Start Sorting" to begin categorisation
  • Ask clients to sort cards into three piles: Often , Sometimes, Never
  • Encourage discussion throughout the sorting process

Helpful prompts:

  • "What surprises you about where this card landed?"
  • "Tell me about the cards in your 'Often' pile - what do you notice?"
  • "Are there any cards you wish were in a different pile?"
  • "What patterns do you see emerging?"

Step 3: Identifying Productive vs. Unproductive Activities (15-20 minutes)

  • Ask clients to identify which activities feel most productive for their decision-making
  • Explore which activities feel unproductive or unhelpful
  • Facilitate reflection on why certain activities help or hinder their progress

Key discussion points:

  • "Which of these activities move you toward clarity and action?"
  • "Which ones seem to keep you stuck or increase your stress?"
  • "How might you recognise when you're engaging in unproductive mental activities?"
  • "What would it look like to engage more with the productive activities?"
  • What are some specific, practical steps you could take this week to intentionally engage more in your productive mental activities?

Benefits for Clients

Increased Self-Awareness:

  • Clients gain insight into the breadth and complexity of their mental activities
  • Visualisation helps them understand why decision-making feels overwhelming

Reduced Isolation:

  • Normalises the complexity of decision-making
  • Helps clients realise they're not alone in experiencing "mental chaos"

Enhanced Decision-Making:

  • Distinguishes between productive and unproductive thought patterns
  • Provides concrete strategies for improving decision-making processes

Empowerment:

  • Transforms feeling "stuck" into actionable insights
  • Validates the mental effort involved in complex decisions
  • Enables clients to take more intentional control of their thought processes

Structural Awareness:

  • Helps clients recognise external factors influencing their thinking
  • Reduces self-blame by identifying systemic challenges

Key Counsellor Notes

  • The categorisation itself is less important than the reflections it generates
  • Allow for spontaneous, open discussion - follow the client's lead
  • Each client's pattern will be unique - avoid assuming typical responses
  • Focus on the client's subjective experience and interpretations
  • This tool works particularly well for clients facing complex life decisions
  • The visual and tactile nature of the activity often reveals insights that verbal processing alone cannot achieve

Remember: This is about putting internal conversations "on the table" to make them visible and workable rather than keeping them trapped in the client's head.
